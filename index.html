Providence, RI to Seattle, WA

Four Thousand Miles Via Bicycle

A photographic Journal

On the 13th of June, Two Thousand Twelve, I left Philadelphia knowing that I would ride a bicycle across the country with my best friend in support of affordable housing efforts. What I did not know, is that my life would be completely changed. I would make 29 other friendships that I would cherish forever. I would make drastic re-evaluations of my values and ideas. And most importantly, it would lead me to make the decision to leave my full-time job and pursue work and life on my own terms.

This is for Bike and Build, Providence To Seattle, Two Thousand and Twelve. For the open road, the adventure, the good times and the tough, the brutal heat and the biting wind, the words exchanged, and the lessons learned. But most importantly, all of you. The ones who were bold enough to go for it, to try something you were not sure you could do. All of us, who were "tougher, or dumber, or both" than the rancher in Nebraska who couldn't believe what we did. To all of you, who did it with a smile on your face. All of you who quietly haunted me when I sat for the last 9 months at a desk and wished I was still on the road.

I read this quote from Jon Krakauer a few months ago, and though it has been shared widely, I think it summarizes my thoughts quite well.

Make a radical change in your lifestyle and begin to boldly do things which you may previously never have thought of doing, or been too hesitant to attempt. So many people live within unhappy circumstances and yet will not take the initiative to change their situation because they are conditioned to a life of security, conformity, and conservation, all of which may appear to give one peace of mind, but in reality nothing is more damaging to the adventurous spirit within a man than a secure future. The very basic core of a man’s living spirit is his passion for adventure. The joy of life comes from our encounters with new experiences, and hence there is no greater joy than to have an endlessly changing horizon, for each day to have a new and different sun.

I'm setting out on an adventure today. A new road to travel. One that is unclear, risky, and I reckon it may contain a good deal of rocks. But I wouldn't have it any other way. I want to live on my own terms. I'll be traveling about the country for a while, I do hope to see many of you on my way. And if you've got that feeling, that itch for the road, that wish that you were not stuck in one place. Then I urge you, to ask yourself what is keeping you there. If you can't find a great reason, then do what your heart tells you to and set forth on the journey once more.

Memories

Anticipating wild weather and tough terrain, I shot most of my photos from the trip on an old Holga camera that I wouldn't be afraid to break or lose. Though the images are a bit blurry, I think they tell our story quite well. A beautiful thing happens when the photo is just clear enough to help you recall the time and place: may your memories do the rest.

Land

There was a moment during our ride in Nebraska, when I had an epiphany about the geography of the United States. I had first thought about following the westward expansion of Europeans, and later about the lives of Native Americans, but at this moment, I realized something greater—we were not just crossing America, we were crossing the continent. Ages upon ages of natural force have shaped the landscape: as we moved into areas with less people, it became easier to comprehend the times when people didn't walk this land. America's beauty was amplified by an awareness, albeit minimal, of the forces that shaped it.

Signs

An item of my particular interest, signs tell stories about our culture, economy, values that are not written in history books. The ideals and attitudes of America have been carefully recorded; we can see our nation's soul on the front of grocers, garages, bars and arenas. Within these mementos, where time has been paused, we can observe the aspects of culture that we have gained and lost.
